The mathematical concept of infinity has always carried a weight of spiritual significance that transcends its technical definition. In Hindu philosophy, the concept of Ananta — the infinite, the endless — is an attribute of Brahman, the ultimate reality, symbolised by the cosmic serpent Shesha upon whom Vishnu reclines in the primordial ocean. The nineteenth-century German mathematician Georg Cantor, who developed the rigorous mathematical theory of infinity, was himself deeply influenced by theological considerations. Cantor distinguished between the Transfinite — the mathematical infinities that can be rigorously studied and compared — and the Absolute Infinite, which he identified with God and which he believed transcended all mathematical comprehension (Dauben, 1979). Cantor's work revealed the extraordinary fact that there are different 'sizes' of infinity: the infinity of real numbers is strictly and provably larger than the infinity of natural numbers. This hierarchy of infinities — each one containing all the smaller ones yet still exceeded by a larger — mirrors the spiritual teaching found across traditions that ultimate reality is always greater than any finite comprehension of it, that the divine is inexhaustible in its depth and richness.
